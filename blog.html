The $140,000 Question: Which Half of Your Marketing Budget Is Working?

Most credit union marketing teams know the answer is in there somewhere -- spread across exports from the core, HubSpot, the call center, and the LOS. The problem is not the data. It is that nobody connected the dots between first touch and funded loan.

Patricia Thomas 6 min read Read full post ⌄

Every quarter, the same conversation happens in credit union marketing departments across the country. Leadership asks what the marketing budget returned. The marketing director pulls together whatever data they can -- usually a combination of Google Analytics, a HubSpot export, and a pivot table someone built six months ago -- and delivers an answer that starts with "based on what we can see."

That qualifier. "Based on what we can see." It is doing a lot of work. What it really means is: we do not actually know.

"The tools to fix this have existed for years. Nobody built them for the way credit unions actually work -- until now."

The allocation is usually based on a combination of what worked last year, what the vendors are pushing, and gut feel.

The Data Was Never the Problem. The Org Chart Was.

Credit unions have more data than they have ever had. The attribution gap is not a data problem. It is a governance problem -- and it lives in the space between the people who own the data and the people who need it.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

Every time I talk to a credit union about attribution, the conversation eventually arrives at the same place. Someone says the data is there. It is in the core, in the LOS, in HubSpot, in the call center platform. All of it exists. The problem is that nobody connected it.

I used to think that was a technology problem. After seven years working inside CU lending and marketing technology, I do not think that anymore.

The real barrier was never the tool. It was a missing framework.

Marketing owns the campaign data. IT owns the core access. Lending owns the funded loan numbers. Each department is doing its job correctly, inside its own lane. But the intelligence that lives between those lanes -- the full picture from first touch to funded loan -- belongs to no one. So it goes unseen.

What I saw across 100+ credit unions

When I was managing enterprise accounts at Origence, I watched this dynamic play out at credit unions of every size. The marketing team would build a campaign. The results would land in a report that said something like "email engagement was strong this quarter." Lending would have their own numbers. The gap between what marketing reported and what lending saw in funded loan volume was real, but it was invisible -- because nobody was in a room where both sets of numbers were on the table at the same time.

The governance problem is not about bad intentions. Every department head I worked with was doing exactly what they were supposed to do. Marketing was protecting its budget. IT was protecting the integrity of the systems. Lending was protecting its pipeline. The problem is that doing your job well inside your own department can still produce a collective failure at the institution level.

The Missing Layer

These meetings have been happening for years. Marketing in their lane. Lending in theirs. IT holding the keys. Everyone giving their side of the story.

The problem was never getting people in the room. It was that everyone left with a different version of what happened -- and went back to their systems with the same disconnected data they walked in with.

So the workarounds multiplied. One-off attribution codes. Single-system dashboards. Vendor tools that solved for one department's visibility without touching the others. Each one a reasonable response to an unreasonable situation.

What the data has always needed is a unified framework. One that pulls every system's story into a single, connected narrative. Not marketing's version. Not lending's version. The actual version.

The data was always ready. It was waiting for the framework to catch up.

You Can't Cut What You Can't See

The data says credit unions that invest in marketing grow faster. That part is settled. What the data doesn't tell you is which part of your marketing budget is actually driving that growth.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

The NCUA numbers are clear. Credit unions above $5 billion in assets grew deposits at 6.1% last year. Those under $100 million grew at 1.2%. The ones that invested in marketing outperformed the ones that did not. By a lot.

So the argument is over, right? Invest in marketing. Do not cut it. The data says so.

Not quite.

Knowing that marketing investment drives growth is not the same as knowing which marketing investment drives growth. Those are two completely different problems. And confusing them is exactly how credit union marketing budgets get defended in board meetings -- and then quietly dismantled anyway, because leadership can see the spend but not the return.

"You cannot sustain the belief without the proof."

The belief gap is real. Smaller institutions still treat marketing as the first line to cut when things tighten. The data says that instinct is wrong. But here is the part that comes next.

A CMO who walks into a board meeting and says "the industry data shows marketing investment drives deposit growth" is making a category argument. A CMO who walks in and says "email campaigns drove 275 funded loans at $291 per loan last quarter, and digital ads drove 12 at $5,000 per loan, and here is exactly what we are doing about it" is making a business argument.

The first one gets nodded at. The second one gets funded.

Spending with accountability

The credit unions that are winning this are not just spending more. They are spending with accountability. They know which channels are working. They know which ones are burning budget. And when the conversation turns to cuts -- because it always does -- they are not defending marketing as a category. They are defending specific campaigns with specific outcomes.

You cannot cut what you cannot see. But more importantly -- you cannot protect what you cannot prove.

The Campaign Audience Trap: Why Attribution Codes Miss Half Your Funded Loans

A marketing director showed me their attribution setup recently. It was genuinely impressive. And then she said something that stopped me cold.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

I was in a demo recently and a marketing director showed me their attribution setup. Honestly? It was good. They were using a segment-based system with a unique member identifier tracking members across email, branch, and call center. They could see one campaign drove six million dollars in auto loans. A third online, a third in person, a third through the call center. Really impressive visibility.

And then she said something that stopped me cold.

"It only works if the member was part of the campaign audience."

There it is. That is the trap.

If a member was not on the list -- was not targeted, was not in the segment -- and they still funded a loan? Invisible. Does not exist in the attribution report. Even if they funded because their spouse got the email and talked about it at dinner. Even if they called the call center after seeing a branch sign.

The household gap in action

A husband calls asking about auto rates. Does not apply. A week later his wife walks in and funds the loan. In most systems those are two separate records with zero connection. The branch loan looks like it came out of nowhere.

Your email campaign looks like it drove 21 loans. But the reality is 34 more loans had an email touch somewhere in the journey -- they just finished through the call center or the branch or came back later through a different channel. Those 34 loans are real. Marketing influenced them. But your system cannot see them because they stepped outside the campaign audience.

That is not a data problem

That is a visibility problem.

Attribution codes are built to follow the members you intentionally targeted. That is what they do well. But your members do not stay inside the lines you drew. They move. They talk to family. They call and then come in. They see an email and apply three weeks later through a different channel.

From first touch to funded loan -- that is the whole journey. Until you can see all of it, you are not measuring marketing ROI. You are measuring the members you already knew about.

Why "Correlated" and "Caused" Are Two Completely Different Numbers

Your marketing team can correlate campaigns to funded loans. Your leadership wants to know which campaigns caused them. This is not a semantic distinction -- it is the difference between guessing and knowing.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

Correlation is easy. You run an email campaign in March. Funded loans go up in April. You write in your board report that the email campaign drove loan growth. Everyone nods. Nobody pushes back. The number is real. The cause-and-effect claim is not.

Maybe loan volume goes up every April because tax refunds drive home improvement projects. Maybe the branch team ran a rate promotion at the same time. Maybe two hundred members who got the email would have applied anyway because their lease was expiring. You do not know. You correlated. You did not prove causation.

"Your leadership does not care about correlation. They care about where to aim the budget next quarter. Correlation does not answer that question."

What causation actually requires

To move from correlation to causation, you need three things: identity resolution (the same person who received the campaign touch is the same person who funded the loan), journey mapping (you can see every touch between the campaign and the funded loan, not just the first and last), and counterfactual logic (you have a basis for knowing this loan would not have happened without that campaign touch).

None of those three things are possible when your campaign data lives in HubSpot, your funded loan data lives in your LOS, your member identity lives in your core, and none of them talk to each other.

Why this matters right now

AI tools are making it easier to produce confident-sounding attribution reports from disconnected data. The language gets sharper. The dashboards look cleaner. The underlying problem -- disconnected systems, unresolved member identities, no journey continuity -- does not go away because the presentation improved.

A report that looks authoritative is not the same as a report that is right. Your leadership will eventually figure out the difference. Better that the discovery happens on your terms, with a real solution in place, than during a budget review.

Identity Resolution Is Not a Technology Problem. It Is a Data Problem First.

Before you can connect a campaign to a funded loan, you have to connect the person who saw the campaign to the member who funded the loan. At a credit union with 80,000 members across five systems, that is harder than it sounds.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

Attribution fails at the identity layer more often than at any other point in the process. Not because the technology for identity resolution does not exist -- it does, and it is quite good -- but because the data that feeds it is messier than most marketing teams expect when they start the project.

Consider what actually happens when a member touches your CU across multiple systems. They open a marketing email via HubSpot under the address they used when they joined fifteen years ago. They click through to your website and fill out a loan inquiry form using a different email -- their work address. They call your call center to ask a question, and the call center rep looks them up by member ID. They eventually apply through the LOS portal. They fund the loan. Your core records the transaction.

"Five systems. One member. Potentially five different identifiers. No shared key. That is the identity resolution problem at a credit union."

What we assess before anything is built

When Attrivix engages with a credit union, identity resolution complexity is one of the first things assessed. How fragmented are your member records across systems? What shared identifiers exist between your core, LOS, and CRM? The answers shape the attribution model and set realistic expectations about coverage from day one versus six months in, as the identity graph matures over time.

A credit union with clean, consistent member data gets to full attribution coverage faster than one with years of fragmented records. The identity layer is where most attribution efforts break down. It is also where Attrivix starts.

30 Hours a Month on a Bad Answer: The Hidden Cost of Manual Attribution

Your team is spending significant time every month producing an attribution answer that leadership receives with polite skepticism. Here is what that actually costs -- and what changes when the answer is provable.

Patricia Thomas Read full post ⌄

Every month, somewhere in a credit union marketing department, someone opens a spreadsheet they did not want to open. They know what the next several hours look like. Exports from the LOS. A data request to IT. Call center numbers pulled separately. All of it landing in Excel, where they will spend the better part of a week trying to stitch together an answer that leadership will receive with polite skepticism.

The answer they produce is not wrong, exactly. It is just not defensible. It is the best possible output from a process that was never designed to answer the question being asked.

"You are not paying for bad attribution. You are paying for an unverifiable answer -- and paying again every month to produce it."

The real cost is not the hours

The labor cost is real -- thirty hours a month at fully loaded rates is money leaving the building for a report nobody fully trusts. But the larger cost is strategic. When attribution is unverifiable, budget decisions default to inertia. Last year's allocation becomes this year's allocation. Channels that do not work keep getting funded because nobody can prove they do not work.

That is not a reporting problem. That is a lending performance problem.

What changes when attribution is provable

When attribution is provable, the monthly report stops being a project. It arrives. Your leadership reviews a document that names the loans, names the campaigns that drove them, and names the cost per loan by channel.

Instead of opening a spreadsheet, you forward an email. Your leadership reads it. Nobody asks follow-up questions.

That is what provable looks like. The question is whether what you are spending today is getting you there.
